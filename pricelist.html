Our Farm to Cup process

1.Harvesting and Washing

The journey begins with harvesting coffee cherries-bright red or yellow fruits from the coffee plant. Each cherry typically contains two seeds, which we know as coffee beans. After harvesting, farmers deliver cherries to a washing station or FACTORY where they undergo de-pulping. This process removes the outer skin and pulp, revealing seeds covered in sticky mucilage.

This mucilage is removed through fermentation, washing, or mechanical scrubbing. This leaves the coffee in its "parchment state" (also called pergamino), with beans enclosed in a thin, papery endocarp. This stage critically determines final quality-if poorly executed (for example,if coffee is over-fermented or beans are nipped), it can destroy the quality of even perfectlygrown coffee.

2. Drying Process The parchment coffee then undergoes drying to reduce moisture content from approximately 60% to around 10-12%. This process happens at the farm for large estates or at coffee washing stations (FACTORIES) for cooperatives.

3. Milling and Grading Once properly dried, the coffee is delivered to a DRY MILL where the parchment layer is removed through hulling, revealing the green coffee beans inside. At this stage, processors grade coffee based on bean sizes-AA being the best, followed by AB, and so on down to T. Coffee traders buys and sells these green beans on the global market or trades them at exchanges like the Nairobi Coffee Exchange.

> Cup: the, as the coffee cools, continue to assess its acidity, body, balance, sweetness, uniformity, and cleanliness. Each of these attributes contributes to the overall score and perception of the coffee

5. Detail: Tally up your scores on the evaluation sheet to determine the coffee's overall quality. This final score helps in making informed decisions about coffee selection for your shop or personal enjoyment.
